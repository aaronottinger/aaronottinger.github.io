Teaching Philosophy

My teaching is inquiry-based and student-centered. While my classes are peppered with lectures, for the most part students can expect to arrive to class with a hands-on activity waiting for them, followed by group discussion and written reflection, often in that order. This process is informed by my research on the history of math, logic, and education. Instead of starting with an abstract discussion, students begin with concrete materials, on which our discussion is then based. Accordingly, students have a chance to work out problems for themselves (with guidance, of course), followed by analysis and philosophical reflection. My classes emphasize formulating questions, thinking about process, discovery, problem solving, creativity, and collaboration.

Creativity in the Classroom

My specialization in media/adaptation studies also informs my pedagogical approach. Adaptations are a creative way to help students transfer unfamiliar information to familiar contexts. In my literature courses, students might adapt a poem from Wordsworthâ€™s Lyrical Ballads to a video game platform that blends text, historical context, and analysis, paired with an essay that explores how the adaptation opens a new point of access. These exercises are also embedded with lessons in metacognition. They help students think about how adaptations allow thinking to migrate across domains. Pedagogical Research and Resources

To bring my love of teaching to others, I strive to balance my research agenda with publications on pedagogy. Plus, most of my social media interactions are related to classroom materials and practice. I have taken numerous classes on online teaching, including a course at Highline's Summer Institute on accessibility, where I focused on accessible syllabi. At present, I am the School for New and Continuing Studies' acting liaison to the Center for Digital Learning and Innovation, where I assist faculty with their online teaching and make recommendations for online teaching across the university.
