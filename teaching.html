Teaching Practice

My teaching is inquiry-based and student-centered. While my classes are peppered with lectures, for the most part students can expect to arrive to class with a hands-on activity waiting for them, followed by group discussion and written reflection, often in that order. This process is informed by my research on the history of math, logic, and education. Instead of starting with an abstract discussion, students begin with concrete materials, on which our discussion is then based. Accordingly, students have a chance to work out problems for themselves (with guidance, of course), followed by analysis and philosophical reflection. My classes emphasize formulating questions, thinking about process, discovery, problem solving, creativity, and collaboration.

Below, I detail some of my practices and provide samples of course materials.

An Emphasis on Adaptation

My specialization in media/adaptation studies also informs my pedagogical approach. Adaptations are a creative way to help students transfer unfamiliar information to familiar contexts. In my literature courses, students might adapt a poem from Wordsworthâ€™s Lyrical Ballads to a video game platform that blends text, historical context, and analysis, paired with an essay that explores how the adaptation opens a new point of access. These exercises are also embedded with lessons in metacognition. They help students think about how adaptations allow thinking to migrate across domains. Courses Taught

At present, I teach in the Department of Digital Technology and Cultures at Seattle University, where I work with new and continuing education students. My classes include Introduction to Digital Culture, the History of Text Technologies, Foundations of Digital Rhetoric, a service learning course on Social Justice and Digital Media, and Multimodal Composition. I also teach digital storytelling for SU's UCOR curriculum. Previously, as a part-time Lecturer, and earlier as a graduate student, I taught literature and composition/rhetoric classes in the English department at the University of Washingtion (2016-18, 2010-16). And for the better part of a decade (2008-2019), I taught first-year writing classes to traditional community college and Running Start (high school) students at Highline College in Des Moines, WA.
